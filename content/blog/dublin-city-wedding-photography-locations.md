Dublin is one of the most photogenic cities in Europe for weddings — and I'm not just saying that because it's home. Between the Georgian architecture, colourful doorways, tree-lined canals, and a coastline minutes from the city centre, there's a portrait location for every couple and every style.

Whether you're getting married in a Dublin hotel, city hall, or a venue across the county, here's my guide to the best places for **wedding photography in Dublin**.

## Georgian Dublin

### Merrion Square

The jewel of Georgian Dublin. **Merrion Square** is surrounded by pristine townhouses with their famous colourful doors, wrought-iron balconies, and perfectly maintained gardens. The park itself is peaceful and green — a calm contrast to the elegant streetscapes around it.

It's also home to the [National Gallery of Ireland](/blog/national-gallery-of-ireland/), one of my favourite indoor wedding venues in the city.

**Best for:** Classic, elegant portraits with an unmistakably Dublin backdrop.

### Fitzwilliam Square

Quieter and more intimate than Merrion Square, **Fitzwilliam Square** is one of Dublin's best-kept secrets for wedding photography. The private garden in the centre is beautifully maintained, and the surrounding streets have that signature Georgian elegance without the foot traffic.

**Best for:** Couples who want Georgian charm without crowds.

### The Iveagh Gardens

Tucked behind the National Concert Hall, the **Iveagh Gardens** feel like a secret garden in the middle of the city. Cascade waterfalls, a rustic grotto, a rosarium, and maze-like hedgerows — all just steps from Harcourt Street. Remarkably few people know about this park, which means you'll often have it almost to yourselves.

**Best for:** A hidden, romantic feel without leaving the city centre.

## Dublin's Canals

### Grand Canal

The **Grand Canal** running through Dublin 2 and 4 is one of my go-to locations for relaxed, natural portraits. The tree-lined towpath, the reflections in the water, and the mix of period and modern architecture create a beautiful, layered backdrop.

The stretch between Baggot Street and Grand Canal Dock is particularly photogenic, with the iconic Huband Bridge and its stone arches.

**Best for:** Relaxed, natural portraits with a mix of city and nature.

### Royal Canal

Less photographed than the Grand Canal, the **Royal Canal** through Drumcondra and Phibsborough offers a quieter, more residential feel. The lock gates and stone bridges add character, and the towpath stretching northward is peaceful and green.

**Best for:** Couples in the north city who want something local and authentic.

## The Docklands & Grand Canal Dock

Dublin's **Docklands** have transformed into a striking modern landscape. The Samuel Beckett Bridge (the harp-shaped icon), the reflections in the dock waters, and the angular architecture of the Convention Centre and surrounding buildings create a completely different feel to the rest of the city.

At blue hour — just after sunset — the Docklands are spectacular. The lights reflecting off the water create something genuinely cinematic.

**Best for:** Couples who want a modern, contemporary feel.

## Coastal Dublin

### Killiney & Dalkey

Just 20 minutes from the city centre on the DART, **Killiney Hill** offers panoramic views of Dublin Bay, Bray Head, and the Wicklow Mountains. The walk up takes 10 minutes and the reward is one of the most dramatic viewpoints in the greater Dublin area.

Nearby, **Dalkey village** is charming and colourful — great for relaxed portraits on the main street or down at Coliemore Harbour.

**Best for:** Dramatic coastal portraits without travelling far from the city.

### Howth

On the north side of Dublin Bay, **Howth** offers cliff walks, harbour views, and a rugged coastline that feels surprisingly wild for somewhere so close to the city. The East Pier is a favourite for portraits, and the cliff path toward Baily Lighthouse is stunning on a clear day.

**Best for:** Adventurous couples who don't mind a bit of wind in their hair.

### Sandymount Strand

When the tide is out, **Sandymount Strand** becomes a vast, open expanse with incredible light. The wet sand reflects the sky, creating a mirror effect that photographs beautifully. The Poolbeg chimneys in the background are an iconic Dublin detail.

**Best for:** Wide, open, minimalist portraits with beautiful natural light.

## Parks and Green Spaces

### Phoenix Park

The largest enclosed park in any European capital. **Phoenix Park** offers everything — deer roaming the plains, the Victorian walled gardens, the Magazine Fort, tree-lined avenues, and open meadows. There's a portrait location for every mood and season here.

**Best for:** Variety. One location, endless backdrops.

### St Stephen's Green

Right in the heart of the city, **St Stephen's Green** is perfect when you want greenery without going far from your venue. The bridge over the lake, the bandstand, and the tree-lined paths are all reliable portrait spots.

**Best for:** Quick, convenient portraits between ceremony and reception.

## Iconic City Spots

### Ha'penny Bridge & Temple Bar

The **Ha'penny Bridge** is one of Dublin's most recognisable landmarks, and it photographs beautifully — especially at night when the ironwork catches the light. Temple Bar's cobblestone streets and colourful facades are just steps away.

These areas are busy, so timing matters. Early morning or late evening work best for portraits without crowds.

### Trinity College Dublin

The cobblestoned **Front Square** at Trinity, with the Campanile and historic stone buildings, is effortlessly elegant. Access can be limited during term time, but when available, it's one of the most impressive backdrops in the city.

## Practical Tips for Dublin Wedding Portraits

### Timing Is Everything
Dublin traffic can be unpredictable. If you're travelling between your ceremony and reception venue with a portrait stop in between, build in buffer time. I typically recommend **30–45 minutes** for a focused portrait session at one location.

### Golden Hour in Dublin
The best light for portraits is during golden hour — the hour before sunset. In Dublin, this varies dramatically by season:
- **Summer (June–July):** Sunset around 9:30–10pm — you'll have golden light during dinner
- **Spring/Autumn:** Sunset around 6–7pm — plan portraits before the reception starts
- **Winter (December–January):** Sunset as early as 4pm — embrace the moody light

### Weather Plans
This is Dublin. It will probably rain at some point. But here's the thing — **rain photographs beautifully**. Wet streets reflect light, umbrellas add drama, and the soft, overcast light Ireland is famous for is actually the most flattering for portraits. Don't fear the rain. Embrace it.

### Indoor Backups
If weather really isn't cooperating, Dublin has plenty of gorgeous indoor options — hotel lobbies, the National Gallery, covered market arcades, or even a well-chosen pub. Some of my favourite portraits have been taken sheltering from a downpour with a pint.
